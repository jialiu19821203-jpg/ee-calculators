Theory

The non-inverting operational amplifier configuration uses negative feedback to control the voltage gain. The input signal is applied to the non-inverting (+) terminal, while the output is fed back to the inverting (-) terminal through the feedback resistor Rf.

The gain is determined by the ratio of the feedback resistor (Rf) to the ground resistor (R1). The gain is always greater than or equal to 1, and the output signal maintains the same phase as the input signal.

This configuration is widely used in analog circuits for signal amplification, buffering, and active filtering applications due to its high input impedance and precise gain control.

Detailed Theory & Application

The Formula

Av = 1 + (Rf / R1)

The voltage gain (Av) of a non-inverting operational amplifier is determined by the ratio of the feedback resistor (Rf) to the input resistor (R1). This configuration ensures the output signal maintains the same phase as the input signal, with a minimum gain of 1.

Practical Tips

When designing non-inverting amplifier circuits, component selection is crucial for optimal performance:

  • LM358: Dual op-amp, low-cost. Suitable for general-purpose amplification and sensing (not a comparator; not for high-speed switching). Works well with single or dual power supplies.
  • TL072: Low-noise JFET-input op-amp, excellent for high-impedance applications. Perfect for audio preamplifiers and instrumentation amplifiers where low noise is critical.
  • OPA2134: Premium audio op-amp with extremely low distortion. Best choice for high-fidelity audio applications and precision measurement circuits requiring superior performance.

Use Cases

  • Buffer Circuits (Unity Gain): When Rf = 0 and R1 = ‚àû (or removed), the gain becomes 1, creating a voltage follower. This provides high input impedance and low output impedance, ideal for impedance matching between circuit stages.
  • Sensor Signal Amplification: Temperature sensors (LM35, thermocouples), pressure transducers, and strain gauges often produce small signals (mV range). The non-inverting configuration amplifies these signals to usable levels for ADC inputs or further processing.
  • Audio Preamplifiers: Used in microphone preamps, guitar amplifiers, and audio mixing consoles to boost low-level signals while maintaining signal integrity and phase coherence.
  • Active Filters: Combined with capacitors, non-inverting op-amps create active filters (low-pass, high-pass, band-pass) with gain. The gain can be adjusted independently of filter characteristics.
  • Instrumentation Amplifiers: Used as building blocks in instrumentation amplifier circuits for precise signal measurement in medical devices, data acquisition systems, and industrial control systems.

  • Supply Rails: Output voltage cannot exceed the power supply voltage (Clipping). Check "Rail-to-Rail" specs.
  • Input Common-Mode Range: Ensure inputs stay within the valid range (often not rail-to-rail; check datasheet limits).
  • Slew Rate: Output may distort at high frequencies or large amplitudes if the op-amp cannot change voltage fast enough.
  • Gain-Bandwidth Product (GBW): As gain increases, bandwidth decreases. (e.g., A 1MHz Op-Amp with Gain=100 has only 10kHz bandwidth).
  • Stability: Ensure your specific Op-Amp is "Unity-Gain Stable" before using it as a voltage follower (Rf=0).
